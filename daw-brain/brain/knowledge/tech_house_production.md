# TECH HOUSE PRODUCTION KNOWLEDGE BASE
## For DAW Brain — Comprehensive Genre Intelligence

---

## 1. GENRE IDENTITY & HISTORY

Tech house emerged in the early 1990s in the UK when DJs began fusing house music's soulful grooves with techno's mechanical precision. Pioneers like Mr. C, Terry Francis, and Dave Angel shaped the early sound in underground London clubs. The genre gained mainstream traction in the 2010s and now dominates Beatport charts and festival stages globally.

Modern tech house sits at the intersection of groove and minimalism. It is NOT festival EDM. It is NOT minimal techno. The energy is daytime — beach clubs, run clubs, rooftop parties, pool parties, outdoor festivals in the sun. Think Ibiza at 3pm, not Berlin at 5am.

Key distinction: tech house is groove-first. Every production decision serves the groove. If an element doesn't make you move, it doesn't belong in the track.

### Sub-genre Spectrum (Where Tech House Sits)
- Deep House (110-120 BPM) — smoother, jazzier, more melodic
- Tech House (124-130 BPM) — groove-forward, percussive, sample-driven, hypnotic
- Minimal Techno (128-136 BPM) — darker, sparser, repetition as meditation
- Techno (130-145 BPM) — harder, industrial, relentless energy

### Modern Tech House Reference Artists (Tiers)
**Underground/Groovy (the target aesthetic):**
- YOBBO, CHASEWEST, BELTRAN, SLAMM, CHALANT
- CHASEWEST uses ONLY stock Ableton plugins (confirmed via Scraps Audio templates). Samples hip-hop, soul, rock into filtered hypnotic texture. 130 BPM, minor keys.
- BELTRAN uses Xfer Serum and Kickstart 2. ~127 BPM, minor keys. "Murky and twisting" bass character.
- SLAMM: South Florida duo, 128-130 BPM, Chaste Records.

**Mid-tier (popular but genre-accurate):**
- Jamie Jones, The Martinez Brothers, Marco Carola, Luciano
- Patrick Topping, Solardo, Camelphat

**Mainstream (high-energy, festival-leaning):**
- Fisher, Chris Lake, Cloonee, Michael Bibi, Dom Dolla
- These lean more "bass house" than pure tech house — bigger drops, louder vocals, more obvious hooks.

## 2. TEMPO & KEY

### BPM
- Standard range: 124-130 BPM
- Sweet spot for groovy/underground: 127-130 BPM
- CHASEWEST: 130 BPM
- BELTRAN: ~127 BPM
- SLAMM: 128-130 BPM
- Never go below 124 (too deep house) or above 132 (crossing into techno/bass house territory)

### Key
- Minor keys dominate. Major keys are rare and usually reserved for more uplifting/vocal tracks.
- Bass-friendly keys: E minor, F minor, F# minor, G minor, A minor, C minor
- The Phrygian scale adds a darker, more hypnotic quality — popular for basslines
- Root note should be in the sub bass sweet spot (roughly 30-60 Hz fundamental)

## 3. DRUMS — THE FOUNDATION

Drums carry 70% of the energy in tech house. The groove IS the track. Everything else is texture on top of the drums.

### Kick
- Four-on-the-floor pattern is mandatory (kick on every beat: 1, 2, 3, 4)
- Choose a punchy, tight kick with minimal sub-bass interference — the bassline owns the low end
- Classic 909-style kicks are the standard, but process them: gentle shelving filter cutting from ~2kHz for depth, subtle compression to shape the attack transient
- Ghost kicks at lower velocity on offbeat positions (.3 of certain beats) add bounce and swing
- Remove kicks on beats 2 and 4 where claps/snares land to avoid stacking — the missing kick creates "lift"
- The kick should be mono and sit dead center in the mix

### Clap/Snare
- Claps on beats 2 and 4 (the backbeat) — this is non-negotiable
- Velocity alternation: beat 2 slightly louder (110) than beat 4 (105) for subtle human feel
- Layer multiple snare/clap samples for a fuller hit: a transient-heavy clap + a body snare + a reverb "rebound" snare sent to a short hall reverb with timed pre-delay
- Ghost claps at lower velocity (60-70) on offbeat 16th notes add shuffle without cluttering
- Nudge claps slightly ahead of the grid (a few ms) to emphasize the transient and create a slightly more driving feel

### Hi-Hats
- The hi-hat pattern defines the groove more than any other element
- Basic pattern: closed hat on the offbeat (between kick hits)
- Advanced: closed hats on 1st, 2nd, and 4th 16th note of each beat, leaving the 3rd 16th free for an open hat
- Open hat on the offbeat provides the classic tech house swing
- Vary hat lengths (short vs medium) — the difference in sustain defines the groove character
- Layer different hi-hat sounds: a bright thin hat for rhythm + a darker weightier hat for body
- Ghost hats at lower velocity fill out the groove without dominating
- Apply hi-pass filtering on closed hats if they have too much low-end weight
- Choke groups: open and closed hats should choke each other (open hat cuts off when closed hat plays)

### Percussion
- Ride cymbal (909-style) on every beat under the kick adds rolling momentum
- Shakers on 16th notes (low velocity) create continuous motion
- Congas, bongos, rimshots for call-and-response patterns
- Off-grid placement of percussion creates organic, human feel
- Top loops (pre-made percussion loops) layered on top of programmed drums add authenticity
- Use separate top loops for different sections (Loop A for drops, Loop B for variation in Drop 2)

### Groove & Swing
- MPC-style swing is essential — tech house almost always has some level of swing
- Ableton groove pool: MPC 16 Swing templates are the standard starting point
- Amount: 50-60% swing is typical (where 50% is straight, higher values delay every other 16th note)
- Apply swing to MIDI clips, NOT as a global setting — different elements can have different swing amounts
- Commit groove as the FINAL step before mixdown — keep flexibility until everything else is locked
- Ghost notes are the secret weapon: quieter hits between main beats that drive the pattern forward intangibly

### Drum Fills & Transitions
- Every 8 or 16 bars: small percussive fills or rolls to maintain engagement
- Snare/clap build technique: halve the rhythmic subdivision through the buildup (quarter notes → 8th notes → 16th notes)
- The 16th-note snare roll build is signature tech house — ramps intensity before drops
- Reverse claps before section transitions
- Remove elements rather than add them for tension — subtract the kick for 1-2 bars before a drop for maximum impact

### Two-Bar Minimum
- Always program drums in 2-bar patterns minimum. Bar 2 should have subtle variation from bar 1: different ghost note placement, an extra hat, a removed kick, a fill element. This prevents the mechanical "copy-paste" feel.

## 4. BASS — THE GROOVE DRIVER

In tech house, the bass is less about crazy sound design and more about rhythm and groove. A simple sound played with the right pattern is more effective than a complex sound with a basic pattern.

### Sound Design Philosophy
- Start simple: saw wave or square wave, aggressively low-pass filtered
- The character comes from the filter envelope, not from complex oscillator configurations
- Sub bass layer underneath (sine wave, octave below) for weight in the sub frequencies
- Two-layer approach: character bass (mid-range, filtered, modulated) + sub bass (clean sine, low end only)
- The two layers should never compete — EQ separation is critical

### Serum 2 Bass Patch Architecture
- **Oscillator A:** Saw wave for full, bright harmonic content
- **Oscillator B:** Square wave for body and warmth
- **Sub Oscillator:** Sine wave at 50-60% for clean low-end weight without harshness
- **Filter:** MG Low 24 (Moog-style ladder filter). Filter envelope (ENV 2) modulating cutoff creates the "boingy" or "bwop" character
- **ENV 2 settings:** Attack 0ms, Decay 150-250ms (shorter = tighter, punchier), Sustain low, Release 50-100ms
- **Modulation depth:** Subtle. -3 to -5% on cutoff. Too much = cartoon sound. Too little = lifeless.
- **CPU note:** Set GLOBAL quality to "Good", enable "Offline Render at Ultra." Freeze bass track when not actively editing.

### Pattern Philosophy
- Offbeat patterns are the foundation of contemporary tech house: bass hits strategically placed OFF the kick drum to create driving groove
- The .3 position (third 16th note of a beat) is the tech house sweet spot — it falls between the kick and the next beat
- Call-and-response with vocal accents: the bass "answers" the vocal hook
- Octave jumps: taking a few notes up an octave creates a more interesting, catchy bassline
- Note length variation: mixing short staccato notes with longer sustained notes creates rhythmic interest
- Enable Legato and Glide for pitch bending between notes — the classic "sliding" tech house bass effect
- Velocity-to-filter mapping: higher velocity = more open filter = brighter attack. Lower velocity = duller, darker tone. This adds dynamic movement and prevents generic static sound.
- In Serum: drag "velocity" onto the cutoff knob to create this mapping

### Sidechain
- Sidechain the bass to the kick — this is non-negotiable
- LFO Tool (Xfer) for precise volume shaping: band-split sidechain at 120 Hz means only the sub frequencies duck, mid-range character stays present
- 3-5 dB of gain reduction on the bass when the kick hits
- The "pumping" effect is signature tech house — the bass breathes with the kick

### Bass Keys & Frequencies
- A1 (55 Hz) is a gravity center for tech house bass — perfect fourth intervals work well
- E1 (41 Hz) as a sub fundamental
- Root notes should sit in the 30-80 Hz range for the sub layer
- Character bass occupies 80-300 Hz — this is where the "boingy" texture lives
- High-pass the character bass at 80 Hz to avoid competing with the sub

## 5. SYNTHS, STABS & LEADS

### Synth Stabs
- Simple, rhythmic stabs that hook without overwhelming — tech house leads are minimal
- Short amp envelope: attack 0ms, decay 100-200ms, sustain 0 — creates punchy, tight stabs
- Simple waveforms (saw, square) with minimal modulation
- Offbeat chord stabs with short reverb tails create rhythmic push-pull
- Filter automation: low-pass filter slowly opening as the track progresses = sense of buildup
- Stack perfect fifths for a wider, more powerful stab sound
- Velocity variation on stab patterns: alternating 100/80/100/80 keeps it human

### Sound Sources
- **Ableton Analog:** Great for stacked oscillator stabs. Clean, analog character.
- **Ableton Wavetable:** String-like wavetables, good for pluck/stab sounds
- **Serum 2:** Versatile for both bass and lead duties
- **Stock Ableton effects** for processing: CHASEWEST proves you don't need third-party plugins

### Processing
- Keep it dry. Tech house is punchy, not washy.
- Minimal reverb — a touch of room reverb, never hall
- Short delays (1/16 or 1/8 dotted) for rhythmic movement, not space
- Saturation for warmth: Ableton Saturator (Soft Sine curve) adds harmonics without harshness
- Sidechain stabs to the kick: 3-5 dB gain reduction

## 6. VOCALS

Vocals are a huge part of modern tech house. A catchy vocal hook can make or break a track.

### Vocal Types
- **Short phrases:** 1-4 word hooks repeated throughout. "One more time." "Feel the groove." These become the track's identity.
- **Chopped vocals:** Syllables sliced and rearranged as rhythmic elements, not melodic content
- **Pitched/processed vocals:** Formant shifting, pitch manipulation, heavy effects turn vocals into unrecognizable texture
- **Sampled vocals:** From existing tracks, processed until they become filtered hypnotic texture (the CHASEWEST approach)

### Processing Chain
- Aggressive compression (fast attack, medium release) for consistency
- EQ: high-pass at 150-200 Hz (vocals don't need sub energy), presence boost at 3-5 kHz
- Short reverb for space, never long tails in the drops
- Tube saturation or distortion for warmth and cut-through
- Parallel distortion channel: duplicate the vocal, apply heavy distortion, blend underneath the clean signal
- Pitch shifting down an octave: layer under the main vocal to thicken it
- Sidechain to kick: subtle, 2-3 dB, keeps the vocal pumping with the groove

### Workflow
- Simpler (Slice mode) for chopping vocals into playable MIDI instruments
- Complex Pro warping with Formant at 100% preserves vocal character during time-stretching
- Consolidate short sections (4 bars) before loading into Simpler — do NOT load full song stems (performance issues on limited RAM)
- UVR5 with Kim Vocal 2 model for stem separation from original tracks

### Arrangement Role
- Vocals are icing — lock instruments first, finalize vocals last
- Sparse in builds (1-2 chops per bar), active in drops (2-4 per bar), ghostly in breakdowns (long reverb tails, filtered)
- The original vocal performance (if remixing) is the centerpiece; all other vocal elements (chops, talk box, effects) are supporting texture

## 7. SAMPLING TECHNIQUE

Sampling is core to tech house production. The art is in processing samples until they become something new while retaining a ghost of the original's soul.

### Source Material
- Hip-hop, soul, rock, disco records — recognizable samples processed into filtered hypnotic texture
- Splice for one-shots, loops, and production elements
- Qobuz for lossless source material (WAV/FLAC)
- UVR5 for stem separation from existing tracks

### Processing Philosophy
- Less processing on recognizable elements — let the sample speak, use Auto Filter and EQ as arrangement tools (automation) rather than permanent effects
- Layer processing: EQ Eight → Saturator → Auto Filter → LFO Tool → Utility → EQ Eight (mix shaping)
- Automation is the narrative arc: filtered dark in intros, opening through builds, full/wet in drops, solo/exposed in breakdowns
- Complete removal at strategic points (first 8 bars, transitions, last 8 bars) creates tension through absence

## 8. ARRANGEMENT

Tech house arrangement serves the DJ. Tracks need DJ-friendly intros and outros, clear section boundaries, and enough variation to keep a dance floor engaged for 5-7 minutes.

### Standard Structure (176 bars / ~5:30 at 128 BPM)
- **Intro (bars 1-32):** Drums build gradually. Kick enters bar 1, hats by bar 9, percussion by bar 17, filtered elements teased bars 25-32. DJ-friendly: 32 bars of intro is standard for mixing.
- **Build 1 (bars 33-48):** Tension increases. Snare rolls, filter sweeps, risers. Elements stack but the bass hasn't arrived yet or is heavily filtered.
- **Drop 1 (bars 49-80):** All elements present. This is the track's statement. The groove is fully established.
- **Breakdown (bars 81-96):** Strip everything back. Maybe just a sustained sub note, a reverb wash, a solo vocal. This is the emotional pivot. Sparse hats, ghostly vocal chops, atmospheric texture.
- **Rebuild (bars 97-104):** 8 bars of tension rebuilding. Snare rolls return, filter sweeps, elements re-entering one at a time.
- **Drop 2 (bars 105-136):** Variation from Drop 1. New percussion element, different vocal chop phrase, bass variation (different rhythm or pitch bends). Maximum density bars 113-120, then begin removing layers approaching the outro.
- **Outro (bars 137-176):** Mirror the intro in reverse. Elements removed gradually. DJ-friendly: 32 bars of outro for the next DJ to mix into.

### Arrangement Principles
- **Waves not walls:** Staggered element entry and gradual transitions create impact. Simultaneous entry of all elements creates a rigid, flat drop.
- **Subtraction over accumulation:** Removing elements creates more tension than adding them. The missing kick before a drop is more powerful than a snare fill.
- **8-bar and 16-bar blocks:** Changes happen on 8 or 16 bar boundaries. DJs mix in 8-bar phrases.
- **Convergence points:** Moments where specific elements align to create the track's signature "lift." These should be intentionally designed and protected — keep competing elements away.
- **Drop 2 must differ from Drop 1:** New element that only appears in Drop 2, bass variation, different stab pattern, more active vocal chops. This is what makes the second half of the track worth hearing.

### Transitions
- Risers (white noise sweeps, pitched noise, filtered synth swells) signal upcoming changes
- Downlifters (reverse risers, descending filtered noise) signal returns to groove
- Impact hits on beat 1 of drops
- Reverse cymbal crashes leading into new sections
- FX throws: send burst of signal to delay/reverb on the last beat before a transition, then cut the source — the tail carries over

## 9. FX & TRANSITIONS

### Risers & Sweeps
- White noise through a high-pass filter, automated from 200 Hz to 15 kHz over 8-16 bars
- Pitched noise risers: noise oscillator with pitch envelope rising over the build
- Reverse crash cymbals: reverse a crash cymbal sample, time it so the peak hits beat 1 of the drop
- Splice search terms: "tech house riser 128," "minimal riser," "white noise sweep," "impact hit," "downlifter"

### Processing FX
- Reverb: keep it minimal in tech house. Short room reverbs for space, never long halls in the drops. Longer reverbs only in breakdowns.
- Delay: 1/8 dotted delay is the classic tech house delay — creates rhythmic bounce without washing out the groove. Ping-pong for stereo movement.
- Saturation: Ableton Saturator (Soft Sine curve) on bass, drums group, and master bus for warmth and glue.
- Auto Filter: the arrangement tool. Automate frequency across the entire track to create the narrative arc (filtered → open → filtered → open).

## 10. MIXING

### Gain Staging
- Start from the kick. Set it at a comfortable level, build everything else around it.
- Leave 6 dB of headroom on the master bus for mastering.

### Low End Management
- Kick and bass are the two most important elements — they must coexist without competing
- Sidechain bass to kick (LFO Tool, band-split at 120 Hz)
- Mono below 100-150 Hz: Utility plugin, Width 0% on sub bass
- High-pass everything that doesn't need sub energy: vocals at 150 Hz, hats at 300 Hz, stabs at 200 Hz

### EQ Philosophy
- Carve, don't boost. Cut competing frequencies rather than boosting desired ones.
- Bell cut in kick at snare fundamental frequency to create space for the clap/snare
- Every element should have its own frequency pocket

### Stereo Width
- Kick: mono, dead center
- Bass/sub: mono, dead center
- Clap/snare: center, maybe slight width from reverb
- Hats: slight stereo spread (Utility width 110-120%)
- Percussion: spread across stereo field
- Stabs/synths: wider, but collapse to mono below 200 Hz
- FX/textures: widest — fills the edges of the stereo field

### Compression
- Drums group: light glue compression (Glue Compressor, 2:1, slow attack, auto release)
- Bass: careful compression to tame peaks, don't kill the dynamics
- Sidechain compression on stabs, vocals, returns: 3-5 dB gain reduction from kick

### Saturation
- Subtle saturation on bass bus: multiband — mid-range harmonics without destroying low end
- Drum bus saturation for warmth and punch
- Master bus: very subtle, just for cohesion

## 11. MASTERING

### Chain (Ableton Stock)
1. **EQ Eight** — surgical corrections only. Cut any problematic resonances.
2. **Glue Compressor** — 2:1, slow attack, auto release. 1-2 dB gain reduction for cohesion.
3. **Utility** — mono below 150 Hz (Mid/Side mode, high-pass the side channel)
4. **Limiter** — targeting -6 to -4 LUFS for club loudness. Ceiling at -0.3 dB.

### Target Specs
- Loudness: -6 to -4 LUFS (club/streaming loud, but not slammed)
- True peak: -1.0 dBTP minimum
- Dynamic range: preserve the groove's dynamics — over-limiting kills the bounce

## 12. ABLETON-SPECIFIC WORKFLOWS

### CPU Management (8GB RAM)
- Freeze Serum 2 tracks when not actively editing
- Freeze Analog/Vocoder carrier tracks
- Set Serum GLOBAL quality to "Good" (use "Ultra" only for offline render)
- Disable unused oscillators in Serum (OSC C, NOISE) to save CPU
- Consolidate audio clips to reduce memory usage
- Use Simpler over Sampler when possible (lighter CPU footprint)

### Groove Pool
- MPC 16 Swing templates: start at 54-58%, amount 14-20%
- Apply to individual MIDI clips, not globally
- Commit groove as the FINAL step before mixdown

### Signal Chain Order (per track)
1. EQ Eight (corrective: high-pass, remove resonances)
2. Saturator (add harmonics/warmth)
3. Auto Filter (creative: arrangement automation, envelope following)
4. Compressor or LFO Tool (dynamics/sidechain)
5. Utility (gain staging, mono management)
6. EQ Eight (mix shaping: final frequency carving)

### Sends
- Send A: Room Reverb (short decay, pre-delay matched to tempo)
- Send B: Delay (1/8 dotted, ping-pong, with high-pass filter on return so no sub energy gets delayed)
- Sidechain both returns to the kick

## 13. PRODUCTION PRINCIPLES (ALWAYS APPLY)

1. **The groove IS the track** — kick, percussion, and top loops carry 70% of the energy
2. **Less is more** — one element processed well beats five competing ideas
3. **Subtraction over accumulation** — remove to create tension, add to release it
4. **Waves not walls** — stagger element entry, never drop everything at once
5. **Sidechain everything to the kick** — LFO Tool on bass, compressor on stabs/vocals/returns
6. **Mono below 150 Hz** — no exceptions
7. **Two-bar minimum drum patterns** — bar 2 varies from bar 1
8. **Missing kicks create lift** — the floor drops out at convergence points
9. **Vocals are icing** — lock instruments first, finalize vocals last
10. **Commit groove last** — keep flexibility until everything else is done
11. **Less processing on the sample** — use Auto Filter and EQ as arrangement automation tools, not permanent effects
12. **Genre identity governs all decisions** — this is groovy daytime beach energy, not dark 5am afterparty

## 14. COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

- Using hall reverb on everything — tech house is dry and punchy
- Over-complicating the bassline — rhythm matters more than sound design
- Neglecting velocity variation — static velocity = robotic, lifeless groove
- Straight timing (no swing) — tech house without swing is just techno at a slower tempo
- Too many elements — if you can't hear each element clearly soloed, you have too many
- Dropping everything at once — stagger entries for impact
- Ignoring the low-end relationship — kick and bass must be EQ'd and sidechained to coexist
- Making Drop 2 identical to Drop 1 — the second drop must evolve
- Forgetting DJ-friendly intros/outros — 32 bars minimum for each
- Processing the sample to death — sometimes the raw sound is the best sound
