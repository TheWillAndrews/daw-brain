## 3.1 SAMPLING PHILOSOPHY FOR TECH HOUSE

Tech house lives on samples. The genre's identity is built on taking recognizable source material — a vocal from a hip-hop record, a guitar lick from a soul track, a horn stab from a disco record — and processing it until it becomes filtered hypnotic texture. The original is a ghost: you feel its presence, but you can't quite name it. This is the CHASEWEST approach, and it defines the underground sound.

### The Transformation Spectrum

There is a spectrum from "obvious sample" to "unrecognizable texture." Different genres sit at different points:

- **Hip-hop** sits at the "obvious" end — the sample IS the beat, and recognizability is a feature, not a bug.
- **Deep house** sits in the middle — the sample is processed but still identifiable to someone who knows the source.
- **Tech house** sits toward the "texture" end — the sample is mangled, filtered, chopped, and automated until it becomes a rhythmic or tonal element within the groove. You might catch a syllable or a melodic contour, but the original context is gone.
- **Techno/ambient** sits at the far end — the sample is stretched, granulated, and destroyed until no trace of the original remains.

Underground tech house producers like CHASEWEST, YOBBO, and BELTRAN operate in that sweet spot where a vocal phrase becomes a hypnotic loop, where a guitar riff becomes a filtered texture that evolves across an 8-bar section. The sample serves the groove — it is never the centerpiece competing with the kick and bass.

### Choosing Source Material

The best sample candidates come from genres with strong musical performances:

- **Hip-hop and R&B:** Vocal phrases, ad-libs, melodic hooks. Mac Miller, Drake, Kanye West — these artists' vocals pitch down beautifully into tech house territory. Pitching a female R&B vocal down 3 semitones creates an indeterminate-gender, soulful texture that sits perfectly in a 128 BPM groove (the Duke Dumont "The Giver" technique).
- **Soul and funk:** Horn stabs, guitar licks, bass phrases, vocal runs. These recordings have warmth and harmonic richness that synthetic sounds cannot replicate.
- **Disco:** String arrangements, piano vamps, vocal chants. Disco records at 110–120 BPM warp cleanly into tech house tempos.
- **Rock:** Guitar riffs processed through band-pass filters become unrecognizable textural elements. Distorted guitar through a 300Hz–3kHz band-pass filter sounds like a gritty synth pad.

### What Makes a Good Sample Candidate

Look for these qualities in source material:

1. **Strong melodic hook** — A 2–4 note phrase that is catchy in isolation. If you can hum it after hearing it once, it will stick in a loop.
2. **Interesting texture** — Analog warmth, tape hiss, room sound, plate reverb. These imperfections give character that plugins cannot reproduce.
3. **Vocal phrase with rhythmic potential** — Short phrases (1–4 words) that can loop on beat. "One more time," "feel the groove," a single ad-lib syllable.
4. **Rhythmic element** — A percussion break, a guitar strum pattern, a bass pluck rhythm that can be re-contextualized under a four-on-the-floor kick.
5. **Harmonic simplicity** — Samples with one or two chords work better than complex progressions. A single sustained chord is a pad. A two-chord loop is a progression. Anything more fights the bassline.

### Source Quality

Always sample from lossless sources. Qobuz for FLAC/WAV purchases. Never sample from YouTube (lossy compression artifacts compound through processing). A 16-bit/44.1kHz WAV is the minimum acceptable quality. 24-bit is preferred when available — the extra headroom matters when you are applying heavy processing chains.

## 3.2 LEGAL FRAMEWORK FOR SAMPLING

### Copyright Basics: Two Separate Rights

Every recorded song has two distinct copyrights:

1. **Sound Recording Copyright (Master Rights)** — Owned by the record label or artist. Covers the actual recorded performance: the specific sounds, mixing, and production captured on tape or in a DAW. Sampling the recording triggers this right.
2. **Musical Composition Copyright (Mechanical/Publishing Rights)** — Owned by the songwriter or music publisher. Covers the underlying melody, harmony, lyrics, and notation. Interpolating (re-performing) a melody triggers this right even if you never touch the original recording.

Both must be cleared separately for a legal release. Unlike cover songs (which have a compulsory mechanical license available through Harry Fox Agency or similar), sampling requires direct negotiation with both rights holders. They can refuse permission entirely or demand any price.

### Typical Clearance Costs

- Standard sample clearance: $500–$5,000 in licensing fees
- High-profile samples: $10,000–$25,000+
- Clearance timeline: 3–5 weeks minimum
- Some labels demand co-ownership of the new track's publishing
- Some demand a percentage of all royalties in perpetuity

### The Substantial Similarity Test

There is no bright-line rule for how much transformation makes a sample "original." The law is genuinely unsettled:

**Sixth Circuit (Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films, 2005):** Any unauthorized use of a sound recording is infringement, period. Even a 2-second sample looped once. "Get a license or do not sample." This applies in Tennessee, Ohio, Michigan, and Kentucky.

**Ninth Circuit (VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone, 2016):** The de minimis exception applies — if the average listener cannot recognize the sample, it is not infringement. This applies in California, Oregon, Washington, and other western states.

Every other federal district court has declined to follow Bridgeport. The Supreme Court has not resolved the split. This means the legal answer depends on where you get sued.

### Practical Risk Assessment

**Lowest risk — Royalty-free samples:**
- Splice, Loopmasters, sample packs, Tracklib (pre-cleared). You have a license. Use them freely in released music. This is the only truly safe option.

**Low risk — Unrecognizable transformation:**
- Source material processed beyond recognition (heavy filtering, pitch shifting, time stretching, granular destruction). No reasonable listener could identify the source. Practically safe for release, though technically still infringement under Bridgeport in the Sixth Circuit.

**Medium risk — Bootlegs and edits for DJ sets only:**
- Never distributed commercially. Played in clubs, shared privately. Labels rarely pursue DJ edits that are not sold or streamed. Practically safe, but still technically infringing.

**High risk — Recognizable sample in a released track without clearance:**
- If it charts or goes viral, you will hear from lawyers. Sony Music v. Trefuego (2024) resulted in $803,000 in damages for an unauthorized sample in a viral hit. The Verve lost all royalties from "Bitter Sweet Symphony" for decades.

### Legal Distinctions: Cover vs Remix vs Bootleg vs Edit

| Type | Definition | Legal Requirement |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Cover** | Re-performing a song with your own recording | Compulsory mechanical license (obtainable through DistroKid, Harry Fox, etc.) |
| **Official Remix** | Authorized rework using stems provided by the rights holder | Written remix agreement with label/artist specifying royalty split |
| **Bootleg Remix** | Unauthorized rework using extracted or recreated stems | No clearance = infringing. For DJ sets only, never release commercially |
| **Edit/Re-edit** | Structural rearrangement of the original recording (extended intro, looped sections) | Same as bootleg — unauthorized use of the master recording |
| **Interpolation** | Re-performing a melody/lyric yourself (no original audio used) | Mechanical license from songwriter/publisher required. No master license needed |

### Distribution Considerations

- **DistroKid, LANDR, TuneCore:** Will distribute your music to streaming platforms. They do not check for sample clearance — that liability falls on you. If a rights holder files a claim, the distributor pulls the track and you absorb any damages.
- **Beatport, Bandcamp:** Same liability structure. You are responsible for clearance.
- **SoundCloud:** More lenient in practice for bootlegs/edits, but still subject to DMCA takedowns.
- **Bottom line:** If it contains a recognizable uncleared sample, do not release it commercially. Use royalty-free sources (Splice, Tracklib, sample packs) for anything you plan to distribute.

## 3.3 STEM SEPARATION WORKFLOW

### UVR5 (Ultimate Vocal Remover 5)

UVR5 is a free, open-source tool for isolating vocals, drums, bass, and other elements from mixed audio. It runs locally on your machine.

**Model Selection Guide:**

| Task | Model | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Vocal extraction (clean vocal isolated) | MDX-Net Kim Vocal 2 | Best general vocal isolation. Clean output with minimal instrumental bleed |
| Vocal extraction (highest quality) | RoFormer (BS-RoFormer) | Newer architecture, more natural high-end, preserves cymbal air. Slower processing |
| Instrumental isolation (remove vocals) | MDX-Net Inst HQ 3 | Clean instrumental with minimal vocal remnants |
| Multi-stem separation (vocal + drums + bass + other) | Demucs v4 (htdemucs_ft) | Best for splitting into 4 stems simultaneously |
| De-reverb / de-echo (pre-processing) | UVR De-Echo/De-Reverb preset | Run this FIRST on reverb-heavy recordings, then run vocal separation as second pass |
| Lead vocal vs background vocals | Ensemble: Kim Vocal 1 + Karaoke 1 + Karaoke 2 | Multi-pass for separating lead from backing |

**Settings for Best Quality (single model, MDX-Net):**
- Segment Size: 2240
- Overlap: 0.99 (extremely slow but near-perfect output — on your Intel Mac, expect 10–15 minutes per track)
- GPU Conversion: Disabled (your Intel Mac has no compatible GPU acceleration)

**Settings for Fast Draft (single model, MDX-Net):**
- Segment Size: 512
- Overlap: 0.75
- Processing time: 2–4 minutes per track

**Multi-Pass Pipeline for Cleanest Vocals:**
1. Run RoFormer first pass (natural-sounding split, preserves high-end detail)
2. Run MDX-Net Voc_FT second pass on the RoFormer output (trims remaining backing leaks)
3. Result: surgical vocal isolation with minimal artifacts

### Ableton Live 12.3+ Native Stem Separation

Right-click any audio clip in Session or Arrangement View → **Separate Stems to New Audio Tracks**. Outputs four stems on new tracks: Vocals, Drums, Bass, Other.

**High Speed Mode:**
- All four stems separated in a single pass
- Faster processing (2–5 minutes on Intel Mac depending on track length)
- Acceptable quality for bootlegs and quick edits
- This is the default mode

**High Quality Mode:**
- Vocals, Drums, and Bass each get their own dedicated separation pass. "Other" is derived from the remainder
- Significantly slower (8–15 minutes on your Intel Mac)
- Better isolation, less bleed between stems
- Enable via Options.txt: add the line `-_Feature.StemSeparation.HighQuality=True`
- **Important:** Close all other applications during High Quality processing on 8GB RAM. This mode is memory-intensive

**Technology:** Powered by Music AI (Moises). All processing is local — no cloud uploads.

### When to Use UVR5 vs Ableton's Built-In

| Scenario | Use | Why |
|----------|-----|-----|
| Quick bootleg sketch | Ableton built-in (High Speed) | Fastest workflow — right-click and go |
| Clean vocal for official remix | UVR5 with RoFormer + Kim Vocal 2 two-pass | Highest vocal quality, least artifacts |
| Isolating drums only | Ableton built-in (High Quality) | Drum separation is strong in Ableton's model |
| Isolating bass only | UVR5 with Demucs v4 | Demucs handles bass/other separation better |
| Reverb-heavy source material | UVR5 De-Reverb first, then separate | Ableton has no de-reverb pre-processing step |
| Maximum control over quality settings | UVR5 | Model selection, overlap, segment size all configurable |

### Post-Separation Cleanup

Separated stems always have artifacts. Clean them up before using in production:

1. **EQ Eight (corrective):** High-pass the vocal stem at 120 Hz to remove low-end rumble bleed. Low-pass the bass stem at 5 kHz to remove high-frequency ghost artifacts. High-pass the drum stem at 40 Hz to remove sub bleed from the kick.
2. **Gate (Ableton Gate):** Threshold just above the noise floor to cut silence artifacts between vocal phrases. Attack 0.1ms, Hold 50ms, Release 100ms, Floor -40 dB.
3. **Utility:** Trim gain to match your session levels. Separated stems often come out at different levels than the source.
4. **Listen in solo AND in context.** An artifact that is obvious in solo may be completely masked by the mix. Do not over-clean — every processing step degrades quality.

## 3.4 WARPING & TIME-STRETCHING SAMPLES

### Warp Mode Selection

| Mode | Use For | CPU Impact | Settings |
|------|---------|------------|----------|
| **Complex Pro** | Vocals, full mixes, melodic content | Highest | Formant 100%, Envelope 128 (default) |
| **Beats** | Drums, percussion, rhythmic loops | Low | Transient mode, Preserve Transients |
| **Tones** | Monophonic bass, single-note leads | Low | Grain Size 20–30 (smaller = tighter) |
| **Texture** | Ambient pads, atmospheric content, creative destruction | Low | Grain Size 100+, Flux 80%+ for extreme stretching |
| **Re-Pitch** | DJ-style tempo changes where pitch shift is acceptable | Minimal | No settings — pitch and tempo are linked |
| **Complex** | Polyphonic content where you don't need formant control | High | No additional settings |

### Complex Pro Deep Dive

Complex Pro is the go-to for any pitched or vocal content that needs to maintain its natural character during time-stretching or pitch-shifting.

**Formant Knob (0–100%):**
- Set to **100%** for all vocal work. This preserves the resonant frequencies of the vocal tract — the frequencies that make a voice sound like itself.
- At 0%, formants shift with pitch. Pitch up = chipmunk. Pitch down = demon. This is sometimes creatively useful, but for standard vocal processing, always 100%.
- The Formant knob has no effect if the sample is played at its original pitch. It only engages when you transpose.

**Envelope Knob:**
- Default: **128**. This works for most material.
- For high-pitched samples (female vocals, bright synths): lower to 64–96 for tighter time accuracy.
- For low-pitched samples (male vocals, bass content): raise to 160–200 for smoother tonal quality.
- The Envelope knob has no effect at the sample's original tempo. It only engages when you stretch.

### Warp Marker Strategy

Place warp markers strategically, not on every transient:

1. **On the downbeat of every bar.** This is the foundation. Lock the first beat of every bar to the grid.
2. **On phrase boundaries.** If the sample has a 4-bar vocal phrase, place markers at the start of each phrase to prevent drift.
3. **On critical transients.** The attack of a drum hit, the onset of a vocal word, the start of a melodic note. These are the moments the listener's ear locks onto.
4. **Never place markers mid-sustain.** Warping a sustained note in the middle creates audible artifacts. Place markers only at the start and end of sustained sections.

**Workflow:**
1. Set the clip's tempo by right-clicking the clip → "Set as X BPM" or by entering the source BPM in the clip's Seg. BPM field.
2. Place the first warp marker on the first downbeat.
3. Place markers every 4 bars on the downbeat.
4. Fine-tune individual beats only where they drift off the grid.
5. Less is more — fewer warp markers = fewer artifacts.

### Matching Sample Tempo to Project

- If the sample's original BPM is known: set it in the clip's Seg. BPM field. Ableton handles the rest.
- If the original BPM is unknown: right-click the clip → "Warp From Here (Straight)" to let Ableton auto-detect. Verify by checking if transients align with the grid.
- For drastic tempo changes (90 BPM soul sample → 128 BPM tech house): use Complex Pro for pitched content. Expect some quality degradation beyond ±15 BPM from the original. For extreme stretches, consider chopping the sample into shorter phrases and warping each individually.

### Pitch-Shifting Without Time-Stretching

**Re-Pitch mode** links pitch and tempo like a turntable. Useful for:
- Quick pitch matching where slight tempo change is acceptable
- Lo-fi aesthetic where the pitch wobble is desired
- Zero CPU impact — no DSP processing involved

**Transpose in Clip View** (with any other warp mode): Shift the Transpose knob in semitones. This time-stretches to maintain tempo while changing pitch. Use Complex Pro with Formant 100% for vocals.

### Creative Warping: Texture Mode

Texture mode with extreme settings turns any audio into ambient pads:

1. Set warp mode to **Texture**
2. Set Grain Size to **200–500** (enormous grains = smooth, sustained texture)
3. Set Flux to **100%** (maximum randomization of grain position)
4. Stretch the clip to 4x–8x its original length
5. The result: any audio — a drum break, a vocal, a guitar riff — becomes a shimmering, evolving pad

This is a powerful technique for creating unique atmospheric textures from mundane source material. A 2-second guitar stab stretched to 16 bars in Texture mode becomes an original ambient layer that no one can identify as a guitar.

## 3.5 CHOPPING & REARRANGING

### Simpler Slice Mode Workflow

This is the fastest way to turn a sample into a playable instrument:

1. **Consolidate a short section first.** Select 2–4 bars of audio in Arrangement View → Cmd+J to consolidate. **CRITICAL: Do NOT load a full song stem into Simpler on 8GB RAM.** A 3-minute vocal stem loaded into Simpler will consume 200–400MB of RAM and cause audio dropouts. Always consolidate short chunks first.
2. **Drag the consolidated clip into Simpler** (create a new MIDI track, drop the clip onto Simpler).
3. **Click the Slice tab** in Simpler's interface.
4. **Set slicing mode to Transient.** Adjust the sensitivity (number parameter): start at the middle value and increase for finer slicing or decrease for fewer, larger slices.
5. **Each slice maps to a sequential MIDI note starting at C1.** Play the slices from your keyboard or MIDI controller.
6. **Draw MIDI notes in the piano roll** to sequence the slices in any order. Now you have a rearranged version of the sample, triggered by MIDI.

**Alternative slicing modes:**
- **Beat:** Divides by musical subdivision (1/4, 1/8, 1/16). Use for tempo-locked loops where you want consistent slice lengths.
- **Region:** Equal-sized segments. Use when the source has no clear transients.
- **Manual:** Double-click on the waveform to place markers wherever you want. Use for irregular material or surgical precision.

### Slice to Drum Rack

For more control over individual slices:

1. Load the sample into Simpler in Slice mode as above.
2. Right-click Simpler's title bar → **Slice to Drum Rack.**
3. Each slice becomes its own Simpler instance on a separate Drum Rack pad.
4. Now you can apply individual effects, volume, pitch, and filter settings to each slice independently.
5. This is heavier on CPU/RAM than standard Slice mode. On 8GB RAM, limit Drum Rack slicing to short samples (2–4 bars max).

### Manual Chopping in Arrangement View

For surgical sample rearrangement without Simpler:

1. Place the audio clip in Arrangement View.
2. Set the grid to 1/16 note (Cmd+4 cycles grid resolution).
3. Select the split point → **Cmd+E** to split the clip.
4. Rearrange the resulting clips by dragging them to new positions.
5. Select all rearranged clips → **Cmd+J** to consolidate into a single new clip.

This is the simplest approach for one-off sample rearrangements. No MIDI involved, no instruments loaded, minimal RAM usage.

### Audio-to-MIDI Conversion

Extract melodic content from a sample and re-synthesize it:

1. Right-click an audio clip → **Convert Melody to New MIDI Track** (for monophonic melodies) or **Convert Harmony to New MIDI Track** (for chords).
2. Ableton analyzes the audio and creates a MIDI clip with the detected notes.
3. The MIDI clip loads on a new track with a default instrument (usually a piano).
4. Replace the instrument with Serum 2 or any synth.
5. Edit the MIDI notes — Ableton's detection is imperfect, so manually correct wrong notes.
6. You now have the melodic content of the sample as MIDI, re-synthesized with a sound you own. This is an interpolation, not a sample — different legal implications (see Section 3.2).

## 3.6 PROCESSING SAMPLES FOR TECH HOUSE

### The Master Processing Chain

This signal chain transforms raw samples into tech house-ready elements. Place these devices in order on the sample's audio track:

**1. EQ Eight (Corrective)**
- High-pass at 100 Hz (12 dB/oct) to clear low-end space for kick and bass
- Cut any harsh resonances with narrow bell cuts (Q of 5–8, -3 to -6 dB)
- For the "telephone effect" on vocals: band-pass between 300 Hz and 3 kHz (cut everything below and above)

**2. Saturator**
- Curve: **Soft Sine** (warmest, least aggressive)
- Drive: **3–5 dB** (subtle warmth, not distortion)
- Output: Compensate gain so level matches the input. Saturation should add harmonics, not volume
- Color: On, Depth 0, Frequency 3 kHz, Drive 3 dB (adds mid-range presence)

**3. Auto Filter**
- Filter Type: **Low-pass**, Circuit: **PRD** (warm, fat resonance character)
- Frequency: **600 Hz** (starting point — this will be automated)
- Resonance: **35%** (enough color without self-oscillation)
- LFO: Amount 30%, Rate 1/4 note synced, Waveform Sine (subtle movement)
- **Automation is the key:** Automate the frequency across the arrangement. 300 Hz in the intro (dark, filtered). Slowly open to 2 kHz through the build. Full open (20 kHz or bypass) in the drop. Back to 600 Hz in the breakdown. This filter automation IS the arrangement arc for sampled elements.

**4. LFO Tool (Xfer)**
- Graph 1: Volume sidechain curve — quarter-note rate, smooth duck-and-recover shape. 3–5 dB of ducking. This makes the sample pump with the kick.
- Graph 2 (optional): Filter cutoff modulation — 1/2 note rate for slower rhythmic movement layered on top of the Auto Filter's automation.
- Smooth: 15% (prevents clicking at cycle boundaries)
- Use Shift+click in the LFO graph to draw stepped patterns for instant rhythmic gating effects.

**5. Utility**
- Gain: Trim to sit the sample at the right level relative to drums and bass
- Width: 120% for stereo samples. 80% if the sample is fighting the bass for center space. 0% (mono) below 150 Hz — collapse low-end to mono.

**6. EQ Eight (Mix Shaping)**
- Gentle high-shelf boost at 8 kHz (+2 dB) for air and presence after all the processing
- Gentle low-shelf cut at 200 Hz (-2 dB) if the sample is still clouding the bass range
- This final EQ is about fitting the processed sample into the mix, not corrective surgery

### Auto Filter as Arrangement Tool

The Auto Filter is the single most important arrangement device for sampled elements. The frequency automation curve tells the story of the track:

| Section | Auto Filter Frequency | Effect |
|---------|----------------------|--------|
| Intro (bars 1–32) | 300–500 Hz | Dark, mysterious, filtered. The sample is felt but not heard clearly |
| Build 1 (bars 33–48) | 500 Hz → 2 kHz (rising automation) | Opening up, tension increasing, sample becoming clearer |
| Drop 1 (bars 49–80) | 20 kHz (fully open) or filter bypassed | Full clarity. The sample hits at full brightness |
| Breakdown (bars 81–96) | 800 Hz, static | Intimate, mid-range focused. The sample exposed but softened |
| Build 2 (bars 97–104) | 800 Hz → 5 kHz (rising faster than Build 1) | Opening faster for bigger payoff |
| Drop 2 (bars 105–136) | 20 kHz + resonance bump at 4 kHz | Even brighter than Drop 1 — the resonance adds edge |
| Outro (bars 137–176) | 5 kHz → 300 Hz (slowly closing) | The sample fades back into filtered texture, mirroring the intro |

### Vocoder Technique

Use a sample as the modulator with a synth carrier to create a hybrid texture:

1. Create two tracks: one with the sample (audio), one with a synth (MIDI — use Analog or Wavetable with a bright saw pad, 4–6 voice unison).
2. Insert **Vocoder** on the synth track.
3. In the Vocoder's Carrier dropdown, select **External** and set the Audio From to the sample track.
4. Set the sample track's output to "Sends Only" so you only hear the vocoded result.
5. Bands: 20. Range: 100 Hz–8 kHz. Depth: 100%. Gate: -40 dB.
6. Play sustained chords on the synth track while the sample plays. The vocoder imprints the sample's tonal character onto the synth's harmonics.
7. The result: a synth pad that speaks with the sample's voice. Unrecognizable as either source.

### Formant Shifting for Vocal Character Changes

In Clip View, with warp mode set to Complex Pro:
- Transpose the clip by -3 to -5 semitones with Formant at 0% — the voice drops in pitch AND formant, creating a deep, androgynous, soulful character.
- Transpose by +3 to +5 semitones with Formant at 100% — the pitch rises but the formant stays, creating a higher pitch with the same vocal body.
- Transpose by -5 semitones with Formant at 50% — a middle ground: lower pitch, partially shifted formant, warm and thick.

### Reverb and Delay as Texture Tools

In tech house, reverb and delay are not for creating the illusion of a room. They are texture generation tools:

**Reverb (Ableton Reverb or Hybrid Reverb):**
- Decay: **1.2 seconds** (short enough to not wash, long enough to generate texture)
- Pre-delay: **30ms** (separates the dry signal from the reverb tail)
- Dry/Wet: **25–35%** on a send return (never 100% wet on the insert)
- High-cut at **6 kHz** (dark reverb tail that sits behind the mix, not on top of it)
- Low-cut at **300 Hz** on the return (no reverb in the bass frequencies)
- Sidechain the reverb return to the kick (LFO Tool, 2–3 dB ducking)

**Delay (Ableton Delay):**
- Mode: **Ping-Pong** for stereo movement
- Time: **1/8 dotted** (the classic house delay — creates rhythmic bounce)
- Feedback: **30%** (3–4 repeats before silence)
- Dry/Wet: **20%** on a send return
- High-pass the delay return at **400 Hz** (prevents low-end buildup in repeats)
- Sidechain the delay return to the kick

### Strategic Removal

Complete removal of the sample at strategic arrangement points creates tension through absence:

- Remove the sample entirely for bars 1–8 of the intro. Let drums establish the groove alone.
- Remove the sample for the last 4 bars before a drop. The listener anticipates its return.
- Remove the sample in the breakdown but leave its reverb tail. Ghost of the sample.
- Bring the sample back at the drop with the Auto Filter fully open. The contrast between absence and full presence is the payoff.

## 3.7 REMIX WORKFLOW (START TO FINISH)

### Step 1: Getting or Creating Stems

**Official remix stems:** The best-case scenario. Contact the label or artist's management. Remix competitions (Splice Remix Contests, label-specific contests) often provide high-quality stems. Official stems are multi-tracked and clean — far superior to any extraction.

**UVR5 extraction:** When official stems are not available, extract stems yourself using UVR5. Use the two-pass RoFormer + Kim Vocal 2 pipeline for vocals (see Section 3.3). Use Demucs v4 for drums/bass/other separation.

**Ableton Stem Separation:** For quick extraction when you need stems fast, right-click → Separate Stems. Use High Quality mode for vocals you plan to feature prominently.

**Quality hierarchy:** Official stems > UVR5 multi-pass > UVR5 single-pass > Ableton High Quality > Ableton High Speed.

### Step 2: Analyzing the Original

Before producing a single note, analyze the original track:

1. **Key and scale:** Use a tuner plugin on the bass stem or Ableton's Tuner device. Confirm the root note and whether the scale is major or minor.
2. **Tempo:** Check the original BPM. If it is outside 124–130 BPM, decide your target tempo now.
3. **Structure:** Map the original's arrangement — where do the vocals enter, where is the hook, where is the bridge? Note bar numbers.
4. **Hooks:** Identify the most memorable element. Usually the vocal, sometimes a melodic riff. This is your centerpiece.
5. **What to keep vs what to replace:** Keep the vocal and any element that defines the song's identity. Replace the drums (always — the original's drums are never in the tech house pocket), replace the bass (build your own groove-driven bassline), replace or heavily process everything else.

### Step 3: Building the New Foundation

Build your drums, bass, and groove independently of the original. Do not start by dropping the vocal over a loop — start with the groove:

1. Set the project BPM (127–130 for underground tech house).
2. Set the key to match the original (or transpose the vocal to fit your preferred key).
3. Program your kick pattern (four-on-the-floor, 909-style).
4. Program hi-hats and percussion. Establish the groove. Apply MPC 16 swing at 54%.
5. Build your bassline in Serum 2. Offbeat pattern, sidechain to kick via LFO Tool.
6. The groove should stand on its own as a compelling tech house instrumental before any original elements are introduced.

### Step 4: Fitting Original Elements into the New Arrangement

**The Centerpiece Principle:** Decide which element from the original is the star. In 90% of remixes, this is the vocal. Everything else in your production supports, frames, and serves the centerpiece. The centerpiece dictates the arrangement — where it enters, peaks, and exits determines the track's arc.

**Vocal placement:**
- Introduce a filtered or chopped teaser of the vocal in the intro (bars 17–32).
- Full vocal hook enters at Drop 1 (bar 49).
- Isolated vocal phrase in the breakdown (bars 81–96) — exposed, emotional, with reverb tail.
- Full vocal returns at Drop 2 with a different chop pattern or additional processing.

**Chopped elements:** Take 1–2 bar phrases from the original's "Other" stem. Band-pass filter them (300 Hz–3 kHz), saturate, automate the filter. These become textural loops that reference the original without dominating.

**What stays intact:** The main vocal hook should remain relatively clean and recognizable. This is the element the listener connects to. Heavy processing on the hook makes it a different track, not a remix.

### Step 5: Arrangement Mapping (176-Bar Template)

| Section | Bars | Original Elements | Your Elements |
|---------|------|-------------------|---------------|
| Intro | 1–32 | None (bars 1–16), filtered vocal tease (bars 17–32) | Kick, hats build, percussion enters bar 17 |
| Build 1 | 33–48 | Chopped vocal, rising filter | Snare roll, bass teased (filtered), risers |
| Drop 1 | 49–80 | Full vocal hook, processed "Other" texture | Full drums, bass, stabs, LFO Tool sidechain |
| Breakdown | 81–96 | Isolated vocal phrase with reverb tail | Stripped drums (hats only), sub drone, atmosphere |
| Build 2 | 97–104 | Vocal chops building intensity | Snare roll, filter sweep, everything re-entering |
| Drop 2 | 105–136 | Vocal hook (different chop pattern), new texture from original | Full drums, bass variation, new percussion element |
| Outro | 137–176 | Vocal filtered and fading | Drums stripping down, mirroring intro in reverse |

### Step 6: Making It Your Own

The remix should be a new track that references the original, not a carbon copy with a different kick drum. Concrete steps:

- Remove at least half of the original's stems. Keep vocals and one distinctive texture. Discard everything else.
- Build 100% new drums, bass, and harmonic content.
- Process the vocal: pitch-shift by -2 to -3 semitones for a deeper character, or chop it into rhythmic triggers.
- Match the vocal's volume to the original during mixdown — use the original track as an A/B reference.
- Add one element in Drop 2 that has zero connection to the original — a new synth stab, a new percussion pattern, a new atmospheric texture. This is your signature.

## 3.8 BOOTLEG/EDIT WORKFLOW FOR DJ SETS

### Quick Workflow (30–60 Minutes Start to Finish)

**Step 1: Source and Separate**
1. Import the full track into Ableton (WAV or FLAC — never MP3).
2. Right-click → **Separate Stems to New Audio Tracks** (High Speed mode for quick turnaround).
3. You now have four tracks: Vocals, Drums, Bass, Other.

**Step 2: Keep What You Need, Discard the Rest**
- Solo the Vocals track. Mute the original's Drums, Bass, and Other tracks.
- If the original has a usable drum element (a specific percussion hit, a clap), keep that track too. Otherwise, discard everything except the vocal.

**Step 3: Build a New Beat Underneath**
1. Set the project BPM to your target (128 for most tech house DJ sets).
2. The vocal will auto-warp to the project tempo — verify it sounds natural. If it is warped from a very different BPM (90 BPM R&B → 128 BPM), expect artifacts. Use Complex Pro with Formant 100%.
3. Drop in your kick, hat, and percussion patterns. Use a pre-built drum template if you have one — speed is the priority for bootlegs.
4. Build a simple bassline in Serum 2. One pattern, 2 bars, offbeat. Sidechain to kick.
5. Process the vocal through the standard chain: EQ Eight (HP at 120 Hz) → Saturator (Soft Sine, 3 dB drive) → Auto Filter (LP, 2 kHz start, automate open in drops).

**Step 4: Arrangement for DJ Use**
- **Intro (16–32 bars):** Drums only. Clean kick on beat 1, hats building. This is your mix-in zone. No vocals, no melodic content — just rhythm for the incoming DJ to beatmatch.
- **Drop (32–64 bars):** Full vocal, full production. This is the payoff.
- **Breakdown (8–16 bars):** Strip to vocal + atmosphere. Brief tension.
- **Drop 2 (32 bars):** Full energy, slight variation from Drop 1.
- **Outro (16–32 bars):** Mirror the intro. Drums only. Clean mix-out zone.
- Total: 104–176 bars. Shorter edits (104–128 bars) are fine for bootlegs — they do not need to be full-length tracks.

**Step 5: Key Matching**
- Determine the vocal's key using Ableton's Tuner on the vocal track or Mixed In Key software.
- If the vocal's key clashes with the tracks you mix before and after it in your DJ set, transpose the entire edit by ±1–3 semitones in the master clip transpose. Use Camelot wheel compatibility for harmonic mixing.

### Export Settings for DJ Use

| Parameter | Setting | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| File Format | **WAV** | Lossless. Universally compatible with CDJs, Rekordbox, Serato |
| Sample Rate | **44,100 Hz** | Universal standard. 48 kHz triggers warnings in Rekordbox and may not play on all CDJ/XDJ models |
| Bit Depth | **16-bit** | Universal CDJ compatibility. 24-bit WAV causes issues in Rekordbox and older CDJs. 16-bit is the safe standard |
| Dithering | **Triangular** | Only applies when converting from 24-bit project to 16-bit export. Ableton's default dither type. Never apply dither more than once |
| Normalize | **Off** | Level-match manually in the mastering chain. Normalization can produce unexpected results |
| Master level target | **-6 to -4 LUFS** | Matches commercial release loudness. Your edit should sit at the same perceived volume as the tracks around it in your DJ set |
| True peak ceiling | **-1.0 dBTP** | Prevents clipping on playback systems and after lossy re-encoding |

### File Naming Convention

`Artist - Track Name (Your Name Edit).wav`

Example: `Drake - Passionfruit (WILLANDREWS Tech House Edit).wav`

Keep it clean and descriptive. Rekordbox and CDJ displays have character limits — front-load the important information.

### Rekordbox Tagging and Library Management

1. Import the WAV into Rekordbox.
2. Set the **genre tag** to "Tech House" for filtering.
3. Add a **color tag** (e.g., purple for bootlegs/edits) to visually distinguish edits from official releases in your library.
4. Set **hot cues** at key transitions: Cue A on the first kick of the intro, Cue B on the first kick of Drop 1, Cue C on the breakdown, Cue D on Drop 2.
5. Set **memory cues** at the intro, Drop 1, breakdown, Drop 2, and outro for visual reference on CDJ waveforms.
6. Verify the BPM and key analysis in Rekordbox matches your production. Rekordbox's key detection can differ from Ableton — manually correct if needed.
7. **Never share bootlegs on streaming platforms.** These are for your live DJ sets only. Share via private links, AirDrop, or USB sticks to trusted DJs.
